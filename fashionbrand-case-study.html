Overview
The Brief

A niche fashion brand approached me to design their e-commerce presence from the ground up. Their product is stripped-down, conceptual clothing — not trend-driven, not mass-market. The clothes are austere, sculptural, and rooted in raw honesty.

The challenge: translate that philosophy into a digital experience that feels just as intentional as the product itself.

"Our vision is grounded in raw honesty and a refusal to chase trends. Each piece is stripped to its essential form — austere, sculptural, and unapologetically human."
— Brand manifesto
The Challenge

Most fashion e-commerce follows the same playbook: big hero image, grid catalog, quick-add cart. But this brand isn't most fashion brands. Applying generic e-commerce patterns would actively undermine what makes it compelling.

The site needed to do two things simultaneously: sell clothes, and communicate a point of view.

Key Decisions

Every design decision was tested against one question: does this serve the brand's philosophy, or does it contradict it?

—
Why minimal navigation rather than a traditional header menu?
The brand rejects noise. A sparse, typographic navigation forces focus onto the product. Every element that isn't essential to the journey is removed.
—
Why editorial-style imagery over standard product shots?
Generic catalog-style product photos would disconnect the clothing from its intended context. Editorial framing communicates how the pieces are meant to be worn and felt.
—
Why a dedicated Size Selection screen?
Size anxiety is one of the biggest conversion killers in fashion. A dedicated, unhurried size selection moment reduces friction and returns without cluttering the product page.
—
Why monochrome with the palest possible grey background?
The clothes are the color. Introducing competing hues into the interface would fight for attention. The background should be as neutral as possible — like a gallery wall.

Reflection
What I Learned

Designing for a brand with a strong point of view is different from designing for a generic product category. The brand's philosophy becomes a design constraint — and constraints make better decisions.

What worked
Design system first
Building the component library and establishing typographic scale before designing any pages paid off immediately. Consistency across 11 screens came naturally because the system was already in place. No backtracking, no rework.
What worked
Treating brand voice as a constraint
Every ambiguous design decision became easy once I started asking "does this serve raw honesty?" The brand manifesto wasn't just inspiration — it was a decision-making tool. I'd bring this approach to every project going forward.
What I'd do differently
Earlier user research
I worked primarily from the brand brief and my own assumptions about fashion e-commerce users. With more time, I'd have conducted even a small round of informal interviews to validate that the browsing flow matched how actual shoppers think about discovering and evaluating niche clothing.
What I'd do differently
Micro-interactions
The static screens are clean, but I didn't explore transitions and interactions in detail. For a brand this intentional, the motion design matters — how the size selector slides in, how items are added to the bag. That layer of craft would complete the experience.
